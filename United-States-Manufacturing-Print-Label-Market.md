It involves the use of flexible photopolymer plates that are mounted on rotating cylinders. The ink is applied to the plates and transferred to the label material, which can be a variety of substrates such as paper, plastic, and foil. Flexography is highly adaptable, allowing for printing on materials with varying textures and shapes, making it suitable for diverse industries including food packaging, beverages, and chemicals. One of the key advantages of flexographic printing is its ability to handle large volumes while maintaining speed and efficiency. This makes it particularly appealing for mass-market products that require consistent and high-quality labeling at scale. Rotogravure printing is ideal for large-scale runs due to its speed and cost-effectiveness at high volumes. It is particularly well-suited for long print runs and can handle a wide range of materials, including non-porous surfaces. However, rotogravure is typically more expensive than other printing methods, such as flexography or offset printing, due to the initial cost of creating engraved cylinders. The process involves forcing ink through a mesh screen onto the label material, with each color requiring a separate screen. This method is particularly suited for labels that need to withstand harsh environmental conditions, such as chemicals or outdoor exposure. As a result, screen printing is often used for labeling products in industries like automotive, electronics, and chemicals. One of the primary advantages of screen printing is its ability to print on a wide range of materials, including non-flat surfaces and textiles. It also offers high ink opacity, making it suitable for labels that need to stand out visually. However, screen printing is best suited for shorter runs due to its higher setup costs and the need for individual screens for each color. This method is labor-intensive, which can make it less cost-effective for large-volume jobs compared to other methods like flexography or rotogravure.
